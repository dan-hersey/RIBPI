Section 2: Tax Policy

Q3. Bitcoin Tax Exemption for Small Transactions

📜 Related bills: S2021 (2026) · S0451 (2025)

💡 What this is about

When someone buys a cup of coffee or pays a vendor using Bitcoin, that transaction is currently subject to state income tax — even if the gain is just a few cents. S2021 would create an exemption for Bitcoin transactions under $5,000/month ($20,000/year), meaning small everyday purchases wouldn't trigger a tax event. Think of it like how you don't pay sales tax on some small transactions in other contexts.

Bitcoin is a digital currency that operates on a decentralized network. Unlike dollars, its supply is fixed at 21 million units, and transactions happen directly between people without a bank in the middle. It's been in operation since 2009.

Arguments For

Simplifies tax compliance for small, everyday Bitcoin users. Removes a barrier to adoption that could attract fintech businesses and jobs to Rhode Island. Revenue impact is minimal since most small transactions generate negligible tax liability.

Arguments Against

Creates a tax preference for one asset class over others. Could be exploited through structuring transactions to stay under the threshold. Sets a precedent for further exemptions that could erode the state tax base.

What other states are doing

Arizona's Virtual Currency Tax Fairness Act (S.4808) proposes a de minimis exemption for transactions under $200. Minnesota's Bitcoin Act (HF 2946) would allow residents to pay state taxes in Bitcoin. At the federal level, the bipartisan Virtual Currency Tax Fairness Act has been introduced repeatedly to create a small-transaction exemption.

Section 2: Tax Policy

Q4. State Tax Treatment of Digital Asset Payments

💡 What this is about

Important context: Unlike the federal government, Rhode Island does not have a separate capital gains tax rate. All income — whether from wages, stock sales, or digital asset transactions — is taxed at the same rate as ordinary income. So the issue here isn't about creating a lower "capital gains rate" for crypto; RI doesn't have one for anything.

The real issue is the tax reporting burden. The federal government classifies Bitcoin and other digital assets as property. That means every time you spend Bitcoin — even to buy a sandwich — you technically owe tax on any gain in value since you acquired it. If you bought $10 of Bitcoin and it grew to $12, spending that $12 creates a $2 taxable event. Multiply this by dozens of daily transactions and the compliance burden becomes impractical.

S2021 (Q3) addresses this for small transactions. This question goes broader: should Rhode Island pursue additional simplifications to make everyday digital asset commerce practical?

Arguments For

The current "property" treatment makes it nearly impossible to use Bitcoin for everyday purchases without expensive tax tracking software. Simplifying treatment stimulates small business adoption and keeps Rhode Island competitive. Other states are actively courting digital asset businesses with friendlier tax environments.

Arguments Against

Tax treatment of digital assets is primarily a federal issue (IRS classification). State-level carveouts could create confusion or conflict with federal requirements. Any simplification benefits digital asset holders over people who use traditional payment methods.

What other states are doing

Arizona's Virtual Currency Tax Fairness Act (S.4808) proposes a de minimis exemption for transactions under $200. Minnesota's Bitcoin Act (HF 2946) would allow residents to pay state taxes in Bitcoin. At the federal level, the bipartisan Virtual Currency Tax Fairness Act has been introduced repeatedly to create a small-transaction exemption.

Section 3: Economic Development & Innovation

Q5. Rhode Island Economic Growth Blockchain Act

📜 Related bills: H7413 (2026) · H5564 (2025)

💡 What this is about

A regulatory sandbox is a controlled environment where new financial technology companies can test innovative products under relaxed regulations for a limited time, with government oversight. Think of it like a pilot program — companies get temporary permission to operate with lighter rules while the state evaluates whether permanent regulations are needed.

A Special Purpose Depository Institution (SPDI) is a new type of bank charter specifically designed to serve digital asset businesses — companies that currently struggle to get basic banking services because traditional banks view them as too risky or unfamiliar.

Arguments For

Provides regulatory clarity that attracts blockchain businesses and jobs to Rhode Island. The sandbox model lets the state learn alongside industry rather than regulating blindly. SPDIs solve a real banking access problem for legitimate businesses.

Arguments Against

Relaxed regulations could expose consumers to untested financial products. The state may lack the expertise to properly oversee novel financial technology. Could create regulatory arbitrage where companies choose Rhode Island specifically to avoid stricter rules elsewhere.

What other states are doing

Wyoming pioneered this approach with HB74 (2019), creating SPDI charters that have attracted companies like Kraken and Custodia Bank. Nebraska enacted the Financial Innovation Act (LB648, 2021) enabling Digital Asset Depository Entities. Arizona's sandbox bill HB 2906 passed 57-0 in the House and 17-12 in the Senate before being vetoed by the Governor. So far, only Wyoming and Nebraska have enacted SPDIs.

Section 3: Economic Development & Innovation

Q6. State-Level Bitcoin & Blockchain Study Commission

📜 Related bills: H7956 (2026) · S2198 (2026) · H5810 (2025)

💡 What this is about

A study commission is a temporary group of legislators, experts, and stakeholders tasked with researching a topic and making recommendations to the General Assembly. It doesn't create any new rules or spending — it produces a report. This is a common tool Rhode Island uses to study emerging issues before taking action (recent examples include commissions on AI, housing, and healthcare).

H7956 and S2198 (2026) would create a special legislative commission to study blockchain and cryptocurrency technology — including applications for voting, land registries, public records, and tax policy — and deliver recommendations to the General Assembly. A predecessor bill, H5810, was introduced in 2025.

Arguments For

Low-cost, low-risk way to build legislative knowledge before making policy decisions. Brings together diverse stakeholders (industry, consumer advocates, academics) for balanced recommendations. Several questions on this very survey would benefit from commission-level research.

Arguments Against

Study commissions can delay action on time-sensitive issues while the technology evolves rapidly. Resources spent on the commission could be directed toward existing legislative staff research. Some view it as a way to appear active without committing to policy.

What other states are doing

Colorado passed SB18-086 in 2018 to study cryptography for government records. Mississippi passed HB1214 in 2024 to develop strategic policy recommendations for blockchain technology. As of October 2025, 238 total blockchain-related measures have been introduced across 45 states.

Section 3: Economic Development & Innovation

Q7. State Digital Asset Reserve / Treasury Diversification

📜 Related bill: H6007 — Rhode Island Digital Asset Retention Act (pending 2026 introduction)

💡 What this is about

H6007 (the Rhode Island Digital Asset Retention Act) would authorize the state treasurer to invest up to 10% of uncommitted state funds in digital assets like Bitcoin, with assets held through qualified custodians using secure custody solutions.

State governments hold reserve funds (rainy day funds, general fund balances) typically invested in bonds, money market funds, and other traditional assets. The rationale for adding Bitcoin is similar to why some pension funds and endowments have: it is not correlated to traditional markets, has a fixed supply (unlike dollars, which can be printed), and has been the best-performing asset class over the last decade. The risk is that Bitcoin's price is volatile — it can lose 50% or more in a year.

Arguments For

Diversifies state holdings against inflation and dollar devaluation. Even a 1–2% allocation limits downside risk while providing exposure to potential upside. Signals Rhode Island as forward-thinking and attracts Bitcoin-related economic activity.

Arguments Against

Bitcoin's price volatility creates risk for funds that need stability (pensions, rainy day funds). State treasurers have a fiduciary duty to preserve capital, not speculate. Taxpayers may not be comfortable with public funds in a relatively new asset class.

What other states are doing

New Hampshire became the first state to allow its treasury to invest in digital assets by passing HB302 (May 2025). The Texas Senate passed SB21 (25-5) to create a state-managed Strategic Bitcoin Reserve (March 2025). As of April 2025, 26 states have introduced bills allowing their treasuries to invest in Bitcoin and other digital assets. At the federal level, President Trump signed an executive order establishing a Strategic Bitcoin Reserve in March 2025.

Section 4: Consumer Protection & Regulation

Q8. Crypto-ATM Consumer Protection

📜 Related law: S0016A / H5121A (enacted June 2025)

💡 What this is about

A cryptocurrency ATM (or kiosk) is a physical machine — often found in convenience stores, gas stations, and laundromats — where people can buy or sell Bitcoin using cash. There are approximately 200+ crypto ATMs in Rhode Island.

In 2025, Rhode Island enacted a law requiring kiosk operators to be licensed by the Department of Business Regulation, display clear fee disclosures and fraud warnings, and comply with daily transaction limits. This was in response to rising scam activity where victims (often elderly) were instructed by scammers to deposit cash into crypto ATMs.

This question asks whether the enacted law strikes the right balance, or whether it should be loosened or strengthened.

Case for Loosening

Transaction limits and licensing requirements make it harder for legitimate users (particularly unbanked populations) to access Bitcoin. Excessive regulation could push operators out of Rhode Island entirely, reducing access.

Case for Strengthening

Crypto ATM scams cost Americans over $114 million in 2023 (FTC data). Kiosk fees average 12–25%, which is significantly higher than traditional financial services. Stronger protections could include fee caps or mandatory cooling-off periods for large transactions.

Q9. Private Key Protection & Self-Custody

📜 Related bills: H7957 (2026) · S2196 (2026) · H5868 / S0375 (2025)

💡 What this is about

A private cryptographic key is essentially the password that controls your Bitcoin or other digital assets. Whoever has the key controls the funds — there is no "forgot password" option and no bank to call. Self-custody means holding your own keys in a personal digital wallet rather than keeping assets on an exchange or with a third-party service (like Coinbase or Fidelity). Think of it as the difference between keeping cash in your own safe versus keeping it at a bank.

H7957 and S2196 would prohibit compelling individuals to disclose their private keys in legal proceedings related to digital assets, except in limited circumstances where a public key is unavailable or insufficient to access the necessary information. A public key is like your mailing address (anyone can see it), while a private key is like the key to your front door (only you should have it).

Arguments For

Protects individual financial autonomy — a core American principle. Reduces reliance on third parties that can be hacked, go bankrupt (as with FTX in 2022), or freeze accounts. Aligns with Fourth Amendment protections against unreasonable search.

Arguments Against

Self-custody makes it harder for law enforcement to trace illicit financial activity. Users who lose their keys permanently lose access to their funds (no recovery possible). Third-party custodians provide consumer protections like insurance and account recovery.

What other states are doing

Wyoming passed HB0086 in 2023 specifically to protect individuals from being compelled to disclose private keys. Oklahoma enacted the Bitcoin Rights Act in 2024 with similar protections. Montana and Louisiana have passed related measures. At the federal level, the Keep Your Coins Act has been introduced in Congress.

Section 5: Digital Infrastructure & Sovereignty

Q10. Digital Commons Resolution

💡 What this is about

A Digital Commons Resolution is a non-binding legislative statement that would formally recognize Bitcoin and open blockchain protocols as public digital infrastructure — shared resources that belong to everyone, similar to how the internet, GPS, or public roads are treated.

The Bitcoin network is open-source software that no company or government controls. Anyone can participate, build on it, or verify its transactions. An open blockchain protocol refers to this type of transparent, publicly auditable system. The resolution wouldn't create any new laws or spending — it would simply establish a policy position that Rhode Island views these technologies as public infrastructure worth supporting.

Arguments For

Establishes Rhode Island as a thought leader on digital infrastructure. A non-binding resolution is low-risk and low-cost. Signals to blockchain companies and developers that Rhode Island views their work as public good, not threat. Creates a policy foundation for future legislation.

Arguments Against

Non-binding resolutions have no legal force and could be seen as symbolic rather than substantive. Comparing Bitcoin to public roads or the internet may be premature given Bitcoin's relatively narrow use case today. Could be perceived as state endorsement of a specific technology.

Section 5: Digital Infrastructure & Sovereignty

Q11. Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) & Financial Privacy

💡 What this is about

A Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) is a digital form of a country's official currency — in the US, it would be a "digital dollar" issued and controlled by the Federal Reserve. Unlike Bitcoin, which is decentralized and no one controls, a CBDC would be fully controlled by the central bank, meaning the government could potentially track, freeze, or restrict transactions.

This question asks whether Rhode Island should take a proactive position on protecting residents' financial privacy in the context of digital currencies — essentially whether the state should weigh in on the federal CBDC debate to protect its citizens.

Arguments for state action

Financial privacy is a core civil liberty. A programmable CBDC could theoretically allow government to restrict what people spend money on. States have a role in protecting residents' rights, even when the policy originates at the federal level (as with data privacy laws). Multiple states are already taking positions.

Arguments against state action

Monetary policy is a federal responsibility; state-level action may be preempted. A CBDC does not exist yet in the US, so legislation may be premature. Federal Reserve officials have stated any CBDC would require Congressional approval and privacy protections.

What other states are doing

Florida, Texas, North Carolina, Indiana, and Louisiana have passed or introduced legislation restricting CBDCs or prohibiting their use as legal tender within the state. Over 20 states have introduced CBDC-related bills, making this one of the most active areas of state-level digital currency policy.

Section 6: Mining, Energy & Infrastructure

Q12. Bitcoin Mining & Energy Policy

💡 What this is about

Bitcoin mining is the process of using specialized computers to validate Bitcoin transactions and secure the network. Miners compete to solve mathematical puzzles; the winner adds a new "block" of transactions to the blockchain and earns newly created Bitcoin as a reward. This process is called Proof of Work because it requires real computational effort (and therefore electricity) to participate.

Right-to-mine legislation protects individuals and businesses who run mining equipment — including at home — from being shut down by local zoning restrictions or utility discrimination, as long as they comply with noise and environmental standards.

Arguments For

Mining operations bring jobs, tax revenue, and infrastructure investment. Miners can stabilize energy grids by consuming excess power during off-peak hours. Some miners specifically seek renewable energy sources (hydro, solar, wind) because cheap energy is their primary cost. Home mining is a form of individual economic participation.

Arguments Against

Mining consumes significant electricity — the entire Bitcoin network uses roughly as much energy as a small country. Rhode Island's small geography and limited energy infrastructure may not be suited to large-scale mining. Noise and heat from mining hardware can affect residential neighborhoods.

What other states are doing

Montana and Missouri have enacted right-to-mine protections. Texas has attracted major mining operations by offering cheap energy and grid-stabilization incentives. New York imposed a two-year moratorium on new fossil-fuel-powered mining operations in 2022, taking the opposite approach.

Section 7: General Outlook

Q13. Overall Stance on Blockchain / Bitcoin Policy

💡 What this is about

This question asks you to self-identify your overall approach to Bitcoin and blockchain policy. There are no right or wrong answers — the goal is to transparently represent your position to Rhode Island voters. The five options range from Champion (actively seeking to make RI a leader) to Opposed (believing RI should not encourage this activity), with Supportive, Cautious, and Skeptical in between.

Your answer here, combined with your responses to the specific policy questions above, will inform your grade on the RIBPI Legislator Blockchain Scorecard. The scorecard is public and available at dan-hersey.github.io/RIBPI.

RIBPI is a nonpartisan research and advocacy organization working to shape smart, forward-looking Bitcoin and blockchain policy in Rhode Island. We work across party lines — our current "A"-rated legislators include Democrats, Republicans, and Independents.
